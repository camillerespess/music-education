Music plays an important role in every aspect of life. From development, to relationships, to imagination, the value of music can be seen everywhere.

Music Increases Motor Control

Interacting with music, whether making it or listening, has positive effects on motor control. Hearing music and dancing to it is not only enjoyable for toddlers and adults alike, it’s also a way to sharpen motor function. What’s more, playing and studying instruments only increases motor function skills.

Row Row Row Your Boat, a classic children’s song, engages listeners to “row” the boat along to the music. In doing so, listeners utilize their motor skills.

×

Music Creates Bonds

Music creates community and a sense of belonging, so relationships can certainly be built or strengthened with it. Bedtime songs, as detailed in a 2016 Carnegie Hall report, are an example of this. “When caregivers sing lullabies, they use pitch, rhythm, and lyrics to soothe, teach language, communicate hope and affection, and provide security as a child goes from wakefulness to sleep, or light to dark.”

With soothing, loving, and uplifting lyrics, You Are My Sunshine is a lullaby that supports intimacy.

×

Music Strengthens Communication Skills

As a form of communication itself, music serves as a way for children to process routines, understand structure, and cooperate. Music also strengthens verbal skills and can serve as a way to provide structure to social interactions. Listening to music in a foreign language is a method to strengthen a child’s communication and language skills, while also creating inclusive environments for students of varying backgrounds.

Au Clair de la Lune is a famous French children’s song.

×

Studying Music can Increase IQs

A 2004 study from the University of Toronto found that six-year-old’s IQs had small increases when exposed to once-a-week piano and voice lessons. The children who participated in weekly music education had average IQs three points higher than the other student groups.

Heart and Soul is a common beginner’s piano song and would likely be found in many children’s lesson books.

×

Music Promotes Language Development

Children who study music routinely have increased vocabulary and reading skills. This is because music enhances language development, an idea supported by the Children’s Music Workshop. The group has found that music education develops the left side of the brain, where language processing occurs.

Set to a tune, the ABCs function as both a way to learn the alphabet and engage with music.

×

Music Inspires Imagination

Music immersion gives children the opportunity to develop creativity, invent, and express themselves. As an art form, music is imaginative and creative. If exposed to it, children could emulate the music they hear and are taught and utilize their imagination to create a novel experience.

Riding on a Train is a song that provides listeners with a base storyline that could use their imagination to expand upon.

×

Music Education Supports All Learning

According to PBS Kids , an education in music helps children in other areas as well. Practicing music requires engagement with the brain, ears, eyes, and muscles. The skills developed in those areas of the body can be used in and out of music. In educational settings, music can be used to set a tune to information, like the alphabet.

This seasons song uses music to teach listeners about different seasons of the year and the characteristics associated with them.
